# Definition

> **Lorentz force**
>
> The force acting on a test charge is proportional to $Q$, it is proportional to the speed $v$ of the particle, and it is directed towards the wire, ie it is perpendicular to both $v$ and $B$.
> $$
> \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{mag}}=Q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}.
> $$



> **Current density**
>
> The **Current density ** $\mathbf J$ is defined such that for any piecewise smooth surface $S$​ the current through the surface is
> $$
> I=\int_{\mathcal{S}}\mathbf{J}\cdot\mathrm{d}\mathbf{a}.
> $$



> **Vector potential**
>
> The vector potential is not unique. Any vector field $A$ satisfying $B = ∇ × A$ is called a vector potential for the magnetic field $B$.
> $$
> \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r})=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime}|}\mathrm{d}\tau^{\prime}
> $$



> **Magnetic dipole moment**
> $$
> \mathbf m=I\int_S\mathrm d\mathbf a
> $$



> **Bounded currents**
>
> Bounded currents insides
> $$
> \mathbf{J}_\mathrm{b}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})=-\nabla^\times\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})
> $$
> On the surface
> $$
> \mathbf{K}_\mathrm{b}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})=\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})\times\hat{\mathbf{n}},
> $$



> **Auxiliary field(Magnetic field strength)**
> $$
> \mathsf{H}=\frac{1}{\mu_0}\mathsf{B}-\mathsf{M}
> $$



# Theorem

## Biot-Savart law

> **Theorem - Biot-Savart law**
>
> The contribution of an infinitesimal piece of wire to the magnetic field is
> $$
> \mathrm{dB(r)}=\frac{\mu_{0}I}{4\pi}\frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^{\prime}\times(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime})}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime}|^{3}}
> $$
> where the $\mathrm d\mathbf r'$ comes from the parameterization of curve $\mathbf r'(t)$
> $$
> \mathrm{dr'}=\frac{\mathrm{dr'}}{\mathrm{d}t}\mathrm{d}t
> $$



**Example of infinitely long straight wire**

We can choose our coordinate system such that the wire runs along the $z$-axis, and the point $\mathbf r$, 



then
$$
\begin{aligned}&\mathbf{r}^{\prime}=z^{\prime}\hat{\mathbf{z}},\mathbf{r}=r\hat{\mathbf{x}},\frac{\mathrm d \mathbf r'}{\mathrm d \mathbf z'}=\hat {\mathbf {z}}
\\&\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime}=r\hat{\mathbf{x}}-z^{\prime}\hat{\mathbf{z}},\quad|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime}|^{2}=r^{2}+z^{\prime2}.\end{aligned}
$$
and
$$
\frac{\mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}^{\prime}}{\mathrm{d}z^{\prime}}\times(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime})=\begin{vmatrix}\hat{\mathbf{x}}&\hat{\mathbf{y}}&\hat{\mathbf{z}}\\0&0&1\\r&0&z^{\prime}\end{vmatrix}=r\hat{\mathbf{y}}
$$
thus
$$
\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r})=\frac{\mu_0I}{2\pi r}\hat{\theta}
$$
where
$$
\hat{\theta}=-r\sin\theta\hat{\mathbf{x}}+r\cos\theta\hat{\mathbf{y}}.
$$


**Example of two parallel wire**

Assume we have a wire along the $z$-axis, with a current $I_1$ flowing through it. 

We also have a second wire, parallel to the $z$-axis, in the $x-z$ plane, at a distance $r$ from the first wire. There is a current $I_2$ flowing through the second wire.



thus the force between two wires is
$$
\mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{mag}}=\frac{\mu_0I_1\lambda_+v}{2\pi r}\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times\hat{\mathbf{y}}=-\frac{\mu_0I_1I_2}{2\pi r}\hat{\mathbf{x}}
$$
where:

- $\lambda_+$ is the line charge of the free electrons
- $v$ is the speed electrons move

## Continuity equation and vector potential 

> **Theorem - Continuity equation**
> $$
> \nabla\cdot\mathbf{J}=-\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t}
> $$
> where
>
> - $\mathbf J$ is the current density
> - $\rho$ is the electron density
>
> The Biot-Savart law under differentiable densities $\mathbf J$ is
> $$
> \begin{aligned}
> \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r})&=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})\times(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime})}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime}|^3}\mathrm{d}\tau^{\prime}\\
> &=\nabla \times\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf J(\mathbf r')}{|\mathbf{r-r'}|}\mathrm d\tau'
> \end{aligned}
> $$



> [!NOTE]
>
> For sufficiently differentiable function $f$, $\nabla\times(\nabla f)=\mathbf{0}$​
> $$
> \nabla\times(\mathbf{A}+\nabla f)=\nabla\times\mathbf{A}=\mathbf{B}.
> $$



## The curl and divergence of the magnetic field

> **Theorem - Ampere's law**
>
> **In differential form**
> $$
> \nabla\times\mathbf{B}=\mu_0\mathbf{J}
> $$
>  **In integral form**
> $$
> \oint_{{\partial\mathcal{S}}}\mathbf{B}\cdot\mathrm{d}\mathbf{r}=\mu_{0}\mathbf{I}_{{\mathrm{encl}}}.
> $$



> **Theorem - No magnetic monopoles (Maxwell’s equations)**
> $$
> \nabla\cdot\mathbf{B}=0
> $$
>
> $$
> \oint_{\partial\mathcal{V}}\mathbf{B}\cdot\mathrm{d}\mathbf{a}=0
> $$



## Magnetic dipoles

When $\frac{r^{\prime}}r\ll1$
$$
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r})=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\frac{\mathbf{m}\times\mathbf{\hat{r}}}{r^2}
$$


## Magnetic fields in matter  

The magnetic vector potential generated by a magnetised object is  
$$
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r})=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})\times(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime})}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}^{\prime}|^3}\mathrm{d}\tau^{\prime}=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{M}(\mathbf{r}^{\prime})\times\hat{e}}{r^2}\mathrm{d}\tau^{\prime}
$$

$$
\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r})=\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\int\frac{\mathbf{J}_\mathrm{b}}{r}\mathrm{d}\tau^{\prime}+\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}\oint\frac{\mathbf{K}_\mathrm{b}}{r}\mathrm{d}a^{\prime}
$$

$$
\nabla\times\mathsf{H}=\mathbf{J}_\mathrm{f}.
$$

For many materials, the relationship between M and H is linear, ie
$$
\mathbf{M}=\chi_{\mathrm{m}}\mathbf{H}.
$$
where The constant $χ_m$ is called the **magnetic susceptibility** of the material. 



Then
$$
\mathbf{B}=\mu_{0}(\mathbf{H}+\mathbf{M})=\mu_{0}(1+\chi_{\mathrm{m}})\mathbf{H}=\mu_{0}\mu_{\mathrm{r}}\mathbf{H}
$$
where $ \mu_r=1+\chi_m $ is called the **Relatively permeability** of the material.

> [!note]
>
> Sometimes another constant $µ = µ_0µ_r $is introduced, and called simply thepermeability.
